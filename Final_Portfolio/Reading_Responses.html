Ulises Carrion - Mail Art and the Big Monster from Second Thoughts

The text itself is very well-written and absurdly captivating. I feel very resonated with the idea that “post is not the medium” because it is not required to understand how the postal network functions when creating mail art, and that figuring out the system does not automatically qualify one as a mail artist. I want to draw parallel to the process of making generative art which also relies on an “autonomous system” - artists themselves are not essentially the best people to explain the codes that made the art but it is the process of human intervention and the ideas to convey that matters.
I am also trying to understand the “Is Mail Art unpretentious… Is Mail Art democratic…?” part, as it insinuates that even though Mail Arts circumvents the traditional (institutional?) process/hierarchy an artist needs to pass through to earn visibility, one artist can still outshine another. It almost feels like how social media works today. But I cannot comprehend Carrion’s idea of this part.
Discussion questions:
• What exactly is the “Monster” and the “Castle”? (the institution?) (Speaking in internet slangs does that mean the power of shadow banning) (I am joking though)
• About the receiving end of Mail Arts, what happens if the correspondents interpret the same message differently?

Olia Lialina - STILL THERE Ruins and Templates of Geocities from Lost and Living (in) Archives

Olia Lialina’s STILL THERE: Ruins and Templates of GeoCities from Lost and Living (in) Archives made me reflect on how much the internet has changed since the days of early, DIY web culture. She describes how broken links, archived pages, and missing images serve as digital ruins—reminders of a time when the internet felt more personal and free. Unlike today’s streamlined, algorithm-driven platforms, GeoCities sites were messy, experimental, and deeply individual. Their disappearance, along with corporate takeovers, feels like a loss of agency in how we present ourselves online.

I’ve spent time browsing old GeoCities pages through the Wayback Machine, and there’s something haunting yet fascinating about seeing remnants of forgotten sites—half-loaded graphics, blinking text, personal homepages frozen in time. It makes me wonder: As we move toward polished, templated spaces, are we losing the raw individuality that once defined the web? Or is self-expression just evolving within new constraints, shaped by the platforms we use today?

Bojana Coklyat & Shannon Finnegan - Selections from Alt-Text as Poetry Workbook

“Alt text has tremendous expression potential” – There has been speculation that alt texts are also used for AI training as it makes the image legible to AI. Sometimes we can also see content creators encourage using alt text in a post to let the algorithm do their favor. I find it very interesting that one may not describe the image in a literal way or even they don’t have to write anything relevant to the image, but alt text can reach unexpected audience regardless.

I also find the process of integrating alt text into creative work revealing, particularly in how it forces artists to confront their own visual assumptions. Writing descriptions for my own images, I realized how easily intent and perception can diverge. Going back and forth between image creation and subsequent alt text writing, it very much feels like a self-critique process.

Ramsey Nasser - "A Personal Computer for Children of All Cultures" from Decolonising the Digital

Ramsey Nasser critiques how computing infrastructure enforces English dominance, marginalizing non-Latin scripts and limiting global accessibility. He highlights how programming’s reliance on English isn’t just technical but ideological, reinforcing colonial biases in digital spaces. His Arabic-based programming language, "قلب" (Qalb), challenges this norm, exposing resistance to non-English code as more than a logistical issue—it’s an exclusionary design choice.

This made me reconsider how we accept English as the default in programming without questioning its consequences. Computing is often framed as universal, yet linguistic barriers make it inaccessible to millions. If coding languages embraced multilingualism, how would that reshape global participation in technology? Would it redefine who has the power to create and innovate?

David Reinfurt - Selections from A *New* Program for Graphic Design

Reinfurt’s discussion of Gestalt psychology and its influence on design caught my attention, especially the example of the rotating beach ball as a waiting symbol. It’s fascinating how a simple visual cue can create an intuitive sense of time and anticipation, reinforcing the way our brains seek patterns and context. This connects to branding, where designers carefully craft visual identities that communicate meaning beyond just logos. Paul Rand’s approach, which Reinfurt highlights, embodies this—he saw branding as more than just aesthetics; it was about creating immediate recognition and emotional resonance. Rand’s IBM and ABC logos, for example, distill complex ideas into simple yet powerful forms that remain effective over time.

This made me think about how much of design relies on subconscious cues.

My question is: How can designers balance simplicity and depth in branding, ensuring symbols remain both intuitive and meaningful across different cultural and technological shifts?

Dot Tuer - Beyond the New Media Frame The Poetics of Absence in Vera Frenkel’s String Games

"Frenkel realized a space in between conceptual and technological affirmations of presence. By substituting the predetermined figures of Cat'sCradle with the live-stream transmission of improvised sequences, Frenkel dematerialized the binding properties of string."

What stands out to me in this section is how Frenkel transforms the idea of Cat’s Cradle into something fluid and ephemeral, moving away from fixed figures to something shaped by improvisation and absence. By replacing tangible string with live-streamed gestures, she breaks the expectation of continuity, making the gaps—the narrative ellipses—just as important as the connections. This reminds me of how digital communication often feels: interdependent yet fragmented, where meaning forms in what is left unsaid as much as in what is exchanged. It makes me think about how technology changes the way we perceive presence—can something be felt even when it isn’t physically there, and how well did modern communication technologies do on this matter?

Paul Soulellis - Performing the Feed

I was really struck by how Soulellis frames the constant digital feed as both overwhelming and strangely intimate—it collapses the boundary between public and private, turning consumption into something emotional and immersive. His idea of “publishing like a texture” really resonated with me; it made me realize that I often scroll through content not to learn, but to feel something—falling into that semi-aware, “new boredom” state he describes. It’s unsettling how natural that rhythm has become.

Soulellis also mentioned how we need to resist the state of endless boredom and voiced his concern about if we are prepared to embrace the form of entertainment that is completely automated/generated by algorithms. This part reminds me of the most recent debate of generative AI and its impact on entertainment, intellectual quality of content and most importantly the value of human creators.

It makes me wonder: as our feeds get smarter, what are we really giving up for the sake of convenience? Publishing once meant making an idea heard—carving out space for a voice. But in an age where everyone is constantly posting, is publishing still as powerful, or has it been drowned out by the sheer volume of noise?

Neta Bomani & Sabii Borno - Beyond Dark Matter from Logic Issue 15 Beacons

What really stayed with me was how the zine uses a fictional story—following Gem and her guides—to talk about real issues like surveillance and exclusion. Framing it as a journey made it feel less like a critique and more like an invitation to imagine something different.

“The Way” feels like a metaphor for dominant technological norms or algorithms that subtly control behavior, while “The Beyond” invokes a realm of imagination, ancestry, and alternate wisdom. It’s a reminder that we can step outside these frameworks and define ourselves on our own terms.

“You can’t fix the computer just like you can’t turn back the clock, but you can change what you do now to share the work of building a different future,” The Beyond said. “When you feel controlled by The Way, you can activate the energy of The Beyond to show you that your computer doesn’t have to define who you are and what you’re capable of. Another way is always possible.”

It makes me think: how often do we internalize the limits of our tools without questioning who designed them and why?

Boris Groys - Art on the Internet

"The results of surveillance are sold by the corporations that control the Internet because they own the means of production.... One should not forget that the Internet is owned privately."

That part really stands out—Groys reminds us that despite the internet feeling like a public, open space, it's actually owned and operated by private corporations who profit off our data. The idea that surveillance is not just a byproduct but a business model makes me rethink how we engage with digital platforms. It’s not just about expression or sharing—it’s about who benefits from that interaction. The fact that these companies own the very structure we rely on for communication and art-making adds a layer of control that often goes unnoticed. It raises questions about whether true autonomy in online art is even possible.

What stood out to me is his idea that online art is less about permanence and more about circulation and attention. This made me think about how value in digital art isn’t tied to physical presence, but to how often and how widely it is seen. -- Online art can go viral in seconds, with or without context.

Is online visibility really the main measure of an artwork’s impact today? how does that change what kind of art gets made and shared?
